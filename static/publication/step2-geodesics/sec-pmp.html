Section 3 Step 2 sub-Finsler Pontryagin Maximum Principle

¶

In this section, the Pontryagin Maximum Principle will be rephrased in a convenient form for the purposes of Theorem 1.1. The precise statement to be proved is the following:

Proposition 3.1. Step 2 sub-Finsler PMP.

Let \(G\) be a step 2 sub-Finsler Carnot group with an arbitrary norm \(\norm{\cdot}\colon V_1\to\RR\) and let \(0\leq T\leq \infty\text{.}\) If \(u\colon [0,T]\to V_1\) is the control of a geodesic, then there exists an absolutely continuous curve \(a\colon [0,T]\to V_1^*\) and a skew-symmetric bilinear form \(B\colon V_1\times V_1\to\RR\) such that

  1. At almost every \(t\in[0,T]\text{,}\) the curve \(a\) has the derivative
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}a(t)Y = B(u(t),Y)\quad\forall Y\in V_1\text{.} \end{equation*}
  2. At almost every \(t\in[0,T]\text{,}\) the linear map \(a(t)\colon V_1\to\RR\) is a subdifferential of the squared norm \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\cdot}^2\) at the point \(u(t)\in V_1\text{.}\)
Remark 3.2.

In the sub-Riemannian case, the the squared norm \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\cdot}^2\) is differentiable at every point, and the unique subdifferential is the inner product \(a(t) = \left\lt u(t),\cdot\right\gt\text{.}\) The derivative condition i then gives the usual linear ODE of controls in implicit form

\begin{equation*} \left\lt \dot{u}(t),Y\right\gt = \frac{d}{dt}\left\lt u(t),Y\right\gt = B(u(t),Y)\quad\forall Y\in V_1\text{.} \end{equation*}

Subsection 3.1 General statement of the PMP

For the rest of Section 3, let \(G\) be a fixed sub-Finsler Carnot groupof step 2 with a generic norm \(\norm{\cdot}\colon V_1\to\RR\text{,}\) and let \(u\colon[0,T]\to V_1\) be the control of a geodesic \(\gamma\colon [0,T]\to G\text{.}\)

Consider first the finite time \(T\lt \infty\) case. By Definition 2.9 of the sub-Finsler distance, the control \(u\) minimizes the length functional \(\int_{0}^{T}\norm{u(t)}\,dt\) among all controls defining curves with the same endpoints as \(\gamma\text{.}\) Since a geodesic has by definition constant speed, it follows that \(u\) is also a minimizer of the energy functional \(\frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{T}\norm{u(t)}^2\,dt\text{.}\)

Define the left-trivialized Hamiltonian

\begin{equation} h\colon V_1\times\RR\times\mathfrak{g}^*\to\RR,\quad h(u,\xi,\lambda) = \lambda(u) + \frac{1}{2}\xi\norm{u}^2\text{.}\label{eq-hamiltonian}\tag{3.1} \end{equation}

By the Pontryagin Maximum Principle as presented in Theorem 12.10 of [2], the control \(u\colon[0,T]\to V_1\) can minimize the energy \(\frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{T}\norm{u(t)}^2\,dt\) only if there is an almost everywhere non-zero absolutely continuous dual curve \(t\mapsto (\xi,\lambda(t))\in \RR\times T^*_{\gamma(t)}G\) such that

\begin{align} \xi \amp\leq 0\label{PMP-normality}\tag{3.2}\\ \dot{\lambda} \amp= \vec{h}_{u(t),\xi}(\lambda)\quad\text{a.e. }t\in[0,T],\label{PMP-hamiltonian-flow}\tag{3.3}\\ h_{u(t),\xi}(\lambda(t)) \amp\geq h_{v,\xi}(\lambda(t))\quad\forall v\in V_1\quad\text{a.e. }t\in[0,T].\label{PMP-subdifferential}\tag{3.4} \end{align}

Here \(h_{v,\xi}\) and \(\vec{h}_{v,\xi}\text{,}\) for \(v\in V_1\text{,}\) are the left-invariant Hamiltonian and the associated Hamiltonian vector field respectively.

More explicitly, \(h_{v,\xi}\colon T^*G\to\RR\) is the function defined from the left-trivialized Hamiltonian (3.1) in the natural way by

\begin{equation} h_{v,\xi}(\lambda)=h(v,\xi,L_{g}^*\lambda),\quad \forall \lambda\in T^*_gG\text{,}\label{eq-cotangent-hamiltonian}\tag{3.5} \end{equation}

and \(\vec{h}_{v,\xi}\) is the Hamiltonian vector field associated to the left-invariant Hamiltonian \(h_{v,\xi}\) and the canonical symplectic form \(\omega\) on the cotangent bundle \(T^*G\) by the duality

\begin{equation} \omega(w, \vec{h}_{v,\xi}(\lambda)) = dh_{v,\xi}(w)\quad\forall w\in T_\lambda(T^*G)\text{.}\label{eq-hamiltonian-vecfield-duality}\tag{3.6} \end{equation}

Observe that if \((\xi,\lambda(t))\) is a dual curve satisfying the conditions (3.2)–(3.4) of the PMP, then also any scalar multiple \((C\xi,C\lambda(t))\) for any \(C\gt 0\) satisfies the conditions (3.2)–(3.4) of the PMP. This observation allows the infinite time case \(T=\infty\) to be handled as a limit of the finite time case. Namely, if \(u\colon [0,\infty)\to V_1\) is the control of a geodesic, then all its finite restrictions \(\restr{u}{[0,k]}\colon [0,k]\to V_1\) for \(k\in\NN\) are also controls of geodesics, so by the above they have corresponding dual curves \(t\mapsto (\xi_k,\lambda_k(t))\text{.}\) By taking suitable rescalings of the \((\xi_k,\lambda_k)\text{,}\) there exists a non-zero limit \((\xi_\infty,\lambda_\infty)\text{,}\) which then satisfies the conditions (3.2)–(3.4) of the PMP on the entire interval \([0,\infty)\text{.}\)

Subsection 3.2 The normality/abnormality condition

Condition (3.2) is a binary condition \(\xi=0\) or \(\xi\neq 0\text{.}\) The case \(\xi=0\) is the case of an abnormal control \(u\text{,}\) and may be ignored in the step 2 setting.

Indeed, suppose to the contrary that there does not exist a dual curve \((\xi,\lambda)\) with \(\xi\neq 0\text{,}\) but only some dual curve with \(\xi = 0\text{.}\) In this case, the maximality condition (3.4) states that

\begin{equation*} \lambda(t) ( (L_{\gamma(t)})_*u(t) ) \geq \lambda(t) ( (L_{\gamma(t)})_*v )\quad\forall v\in V_1\quad\text{a.e. }t\in[0,T]\text{,} \end{equation*}

which is only possible when \((L_{\gamma(t)})_*V_1\subset\ker\lambda(t)\text{.}\) Moreover, second order optimality conditions from Section 20 of [2] further imply that (possibly changing the dual curve \(\lambda\)) the Goh condition

\begin{equation*} \lambda(t) ((L_{\gamma(t)})_*[v,w])=0,\quad \forall v,w\in V_1\quad\text{a.e. }t\in[0,T] \end{equation*}

is also satisfied. Since the group \(G\) has step 2, its Lie algebra has the decomposition \(\mathfrak{g}=V_1\oplus[V_1,V_1]\text{.}\) The above would then imply that \(\lambda(t)= 0\) almost everywhere, which would contradict the assumption that \((\xi,\lambda)\) is almost everywhere non-zero.

Therefore without loss of generality it suffices to consider the normal case \(\xi\lt 0\text{.}\) By rescaling \((\xi,\lambda)\) it further suffices to consider the case \(\xi=-1\text{.}\)

Subsection 3.3 The Hamiltonian ODE in left-trivialized coordinates

¶

The normal Hamiltonian vector field \(\vec{h}_{u(t),-1}(\lambda)\) appearing in the ODE (3.3) is straight-forward to compute in left-trivialized coordinates on \(T^*G\text{.}\) The explicit expression will be given in Lemma 3.3.

Let \(X_1,\ldots,X_r\) be a basis of \(V_1\text{.}\) Fix a basis \(X_{r+1},\ldots,X_n\) for \(V_2=[V_1,V_1]\) by choosing a maximal linearly independent subset of the Lie brackets \(\{[X_i,X_j]: 1\leq i\lt j\leq r\}\text{.}\) By an abuse of notation, denote also by \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\text{,}\) the corresponding left-invariant frame of \(TG\text{.}\) Let \(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n\) be the dual left-invariant frame of \(T^*G\text{.}\) Any covector \(\lambda\in T_g^*G\) can be written in the frame as

\begin{equation*} \lambda = \sum_{i=1}^na_i(\lambda)\theta_i(g)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The functions \(a_i\colon T^*G\to\RR\) together with coordinates \(g\in G\) define left-trivialized coordinates on \(T^*G\text{.}\)

Lemma 3.3.

For any vector \(v\in V_1\text{,}\) the Hamiltonian vector field of the normal left-invariant Hamiltonian \(h_{v,-1}\) has in left-trivialized coordinates the expression

\begin{equation*} \vec{h}_{v,-1}(\lambda) = \sum_{1\leq i\leq r}\lambda((L_{\pi(\lambda)})_*[v,X_i])\partial a_i + (L_{\pi(\lambda)})_*v\in T_\lambda(T^*G)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Proof.

Let

\begin{equation} F(v,\lambda) := \sum_{1\leq i\leq r}\lambda((L_{\pi(\lambda)})_*[v,X_i])\partial a_i + (L_{\pi(\lambda)})_*v\label{eq-hamiltonian-vector-field}\tag{3.7} \end{equation}

be the expression on the right-hand side of the claimed formula for the normal Hamiltonian \(\vec{h}_{v,-1}\text{.}\) By the definition of a Hamiltonian vector field, it suffices to verify that the vector field \(\lambda\mapsto F(v,\lambda)\) satisfies the duality (3.6), i.e., that

\begin{equation} \omega(w,F(v,\lambda)) = dh_{v,-1}(w)\quad\forall w\in T_\lambda(T^*G)\text{.}\label{eq-hamiltonian-claimed-duality}\tag{3.8} \end{equation}

The differential on the right-hand side of (3.8) is easily computed. The normal Hamiltonian \(h_{v,-1}\colon T^*G\to \RR\) defined by (3.5) is left-invariant and linear on fibers. Therefore in left-trivialized coordinates, the differential has the expression

\begin{equation} dh_{v,-1} = \sum_{i=1}^nv_ida_i\text{.}\label{eq-hamiltonian-differential}\tag{3.9} \end{equation}

The expression for the symplectic form is more intricate. By definition the canonical symplectic form \(\omega\) on the cotangent bundle \(T^*G\) is the differential of the tautological one-form \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\theta_i\text{.}\) That is, in left-trivialized coordinates, the symplectic form has the expression (see e.g. Section 4.2 of [1] for more details)

\begin{equation} \omega = \sum_{i=1}^nda_i\wedge \theta_i + \sum_{i=1}^na_id\theta_i. \label{eq-symplectic-form-in-coords}\tag{3.10} \end{equation}

The differentials \(d\theta_i\) can be evaluated along vector fields \(X,Y\in\Gamma(TG)\) by the classical formula

\begin{equation*} d\theta_i(X,Y) = X\theta_i(Y)-Y\theta_i(X)-\theta_i([X,Y])\text{.} \end{equation*}

For left-invariant vector fields this reduces to \(d\theta_i(X,Y)=-\theta_i([X,Y])\text{.}\)

Let \(W\) and \(Z\) be vector fields on the cotangent \(T^*G\) such that the projections \(X:=\pi_*W\) and \(Y:=\pi_*Z\) are left invariant. Writing the vector fields in left-trivialized coordinates as \(W=\sum_{i=1}^nw_i\partial_{a_i}+X\) and \(Z=\sum_{i=1}^nz_i\partial_{a_i}+Y\text{,}\) the identity (3.10) gives the expression

\begin{equation} \omega(W,Z) = \sum_{i=1}^nw_i\theta_i(Y) - \sum_{i=1}^nz_i\theta_i(X) - \sum_{i=1}^na_i\theta_i([X,Y])\text{.}\label{eq-symplectic-form-evaluation}\tag{3.11} \end{equation}

The duality (3.8) will now be deduced by comparing the expressions (3.9) and (3.11). For an arbitrary vector \(w\in T_\lambda(T^*G)\text{,}\) let \(W\) be the left-invariant extension to a vector field on the cotangent \(T^*G\text{.}\) That is, the vector field \(W\) has a constant coefficient coordinate expression

\begin{equation} W=\sum_{i=1}^nw_i\partial a_i + \sum_{i=1}^nx_iX_i\text{.}\label{eq-test-vector}\tag{3.12} \end{equation}

Denote by \(X:=\pi_*W\) and \(Y := \pi_* F(v,\cdot)\) the projection vector fields on the group \(G\) of the vector fields \(W\) and \(\lambda\mapsto F(v,\lambda)\text{.}\) Substituting the expressions (3.12) of \(W\) and (3.7) of \(F(v,\cdot)\) into the expression (3.11) for \(\omega(W,Z)\) with \(Z=F(v,\lambda)\) gives the three sums

\begin{align*} \sum_{i=1}^nw_i\theta_i((L_{\pi(\lambda)})_*v) \amp=\sum_{i=1}^nw_iv_i,\\ \sum_{i=1}^n\lambda((L_{\pi(\lambda)})_*[v,X_i])\theta_i(X) \amp=\lambda([V,X])\quad\text{and}\\ \sum_{i=1}^na_i\theta_i([X,V]) \amp=\lambda([X,V])\text{.} \end{align*}

By anti-commutativity of the Lie bracket, the last two sums cancel out, so \(\omega(W,F(v,\lambda)) = \sum_{i=1}^nw_iv_i\text{.}\) Since the expression (3.9) of the differential also gives \(dh_{v,-1}(W)=\sum_{i=1}^nw_iv_i\text{,}\) the vector field \(\lambda\mapsto F(v,\lambda)\) indeed satisfies the duality (3.8).

Subsection 3.4 Conclusion of the step 2 sub-Finsler PMP

Proof of Proposition 3.1.

The curve \(a\colon[0,T]\to V_1^*\) will be given by restricting the linear map

\begin{equation} a(t) := (L_{\gamma(t)})^*\lambda(t)\colon \mathfrak{g}\to\RR\label{eq-subdifferential-curve-defn}\tag{3.13} \end{equation}

to \(V_1\text{.}\) The skew-symmetric bilinear form \(B\colon V_1\times V_1\to\RR\) will be given by

\begin{equation} B(X,Y) = a(t)[X,Y]\text{.}\label{eq-bilinear-form-defn}\tag{3.14} \end{equation}

In order to see that the above expressions are well defined and have the desired properties, express the curve \(\lambda(t)\) in left-trivialized coordinates as

\begin{equation*} \lambda(t) = \sum_{i=1}^na_i(t)\theta_i(\gamma(t))\text{.} \end{equation*}

The curve \(a(t)\) of (3.13) has the same coefficients as the curve \(\lambda(t)\text{,}\) i.e., it is given by \(a(t) = \sum_{i=1}^na_i(t)\theta_i(e)\text{.}\) Using the explicit expression for the normal Hamiltonian vector field from Lemma 3.3, the normal Hamiltonian ODE \(\dot{\lambda} = \vec{h}_{u(t),-1}(\lambda)\) implies that the components of the curve \(a\) have the derivatives

\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \dot{a}_i(t) = a(t)[u(t),X_i], \amp i\in\{ 1,\ldots, r\}\\ \dot{a}_i(t) = 0, \amp i\in\{ r+1,\ldots, n\} \end{cases}\label{eq-coordinate-ode}\tag{3.15} \end{equation}

Observe that the vertical coefficients \(a_{r+1},\dots,a_n\) are all constant, and that \(\theta_i([X,Y])=0\) for \(i=1,\ldots,r\text{.}\) Therefore \(a(t)[X,Y] = \sum_{i=r+1}^{n}a_i\theta_i([X,Y])\) is constant in \(t\text{,}\) so (3.14) defines a unique bilinear form \(B\) independent from \(t\text{.}\) Moreover, the non-trivial equations of the system (3.15) are then exactly

\begin{equation*} \dot{a}_i(t) = a(t)[u(t),X_i] = B(u(t),X_i),\quad i\in\{1,\ldots,r\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Writing an arbitrary vector \(Y\in V_1\) in the basis \(X_1,\ldots,X_r\) as \(Y=y_1X_1+\dots+y_rX_r\text{,}\) the derivative condition 3.1i follows by linearity:

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}a(t)Y = \frac{d}{dt}\sum_{i=1}^na_i(t)y_i = \sum_{i=1}^nB(u(t),X_i)y_i = B(u(t),Y)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The subdifferential condition 3.1ii for the linear functions \(a(t)\) follows from rephrasing the maximality condition (3.4). Namely, expanding out the explicit expressions of the normal Hamiltonians \(h_{u(t),-1}\) and \(h_{v,-1}\) from (3.1) and reorganizing terms, the maximality condition (3.4) is equivalently stated as

\begin{equation*} a(t)v - a(t)u(t) \leq \frac{1}{2}\norm{v}^2-\frac{1}{2}\norm{u(t)}^2 \quad\forall v\in V_1\quad\text{a.e. }t\in[0,T]\text{.} \end{equation*}

This is exactly Definition 2.16 for the linear function \(a(t)\) being a subdifferential of the squared norm \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\cdot}^2\) at the point \(u(t)\in V_1\text{.}\)
