Proof of Theorem 1.1.

Let \(\gamma\colon[0,\infty)\to G\) be an infinite geodesic and let \(u\colon[0,\infty)\to V_1\) be its control. Let \(a\colon[0,\infty)\to V_1^*\) be the curve of subdifferentials of the squared norm \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\cdot}^2\) at the points \(u(t)\text{,}\) and let \(B\colon V_1\times V_1\to\RR\) be the skew-symmetric bilinear form given by the PMP 3.1.

By Lemma 2.15, there exists a sequence \(\lambda_k\to\infty\) such that the blowdown \(\tilde{\gamma}=\lim\limits_{k\to\infty}\delta_{1/\lambda_k}\circ\gamma\circ\delta_{\lambda_k}\colon [0,\infty)\to G\) is a line. By Lemma 2.14, taking a subsequence if necessary, the dilated controls \(u_{\lambda_k}(t) = u(\lambda_kt)\) converge in \(\Lloc([0,\infty);V_1)\) to a constant control \(\tilde{u}(t)\equiv: Y\in V_1\text{.}\) Lemma 4.2 further implies that \(Y\in\ker B\text{.}\)

Since \(Y\in\ker B\text{,}\) the derivative condition PMP 3.1i implies that at almost every \(t\in[0,\infty)\text{,}\) the derivative of the curve \(t\mapsto a(t)Y\) is

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}a(t)Y = B(u(t),Y) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

That is, \(a(t)Y\equiv: C\) is constant.

The convergence \(u_{\lambda_k}\to \tilde{u}\) in \(\Lloc([0,\infty);V_1)\) implies pointwise convergence almost everywhere. That is, for almost every \(t\in[0,\infty)\text{,}\) the vector \(Y\in V_1\) is the limit

\begin{equation*} Y = \tilde{u}(t) = \lim\limits_{k\to\infty}u(\lambda_k t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Fix any such \(t\in[0,\infty)\text{.}\) By Lemma 2.17, up to taking a further subsequence, the subdifferentials \(a(\lambda_k t)\) of the squared norm \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\cdot}^2\) at the points \(u(\lambda_k t)\) converge to a subdifferential \(\tilde{a}\colon V_1\to\RR\) of the squared norm \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\cdot}^2\) at the point \(Y\text{.}\) Moreover, since \(a(t)Y\equiv C\) is constant, also the limit satisfies \(\tilde{a}Y = C\text{.}\)

Applying Lemma 2.19 for the subdifferential \(\tilde{a}\) shows that \(C = \tilde{a}Y = \norm{Y}^2\text{.}\) Similarly applying Lemma 2.19 for the subdifferential \(a(t)\) shows that \(a(t)u(t) = \norm{u(t)}^2\text{.}\) Since the curves \(\gamma\) and \(\tilde{\gamma}\) are both geodesics, \(\norm{u(t)}\equiv 1 = \norm{Y}\text{.}\) It follows that

\begin{equation} a(t)Y = C = 1 = a(t)u(t)\text{.}\tag{5.1} \end{equation}

The norm \(\norm{\cdot}\) is by assumption strictly convex and the map \(x\mapsto \frac{1}{2}x^2\) is strictly increasing and convex on \([0,\infty)\text{,}\) so also the squared norm \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\cdot}^2\) is strictly convex. Then by Lemma 2.18, the point \(u(t)\) is the unique maximizer for the linear map \(a(t)\) in the corresponding sublevel set, so the equality (5.1) implies that \(u(t)=Y\text{.}\) Since the same is true for almost every \(t\in[0,\infty)\text{,}\) it follows that \(\gamma=\tilde{\gamma}\text{,}\) so the curve \(\gamma\) is itself a line.
