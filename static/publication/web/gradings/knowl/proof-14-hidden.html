Iterating the Leibniz-rule, one finds that the derivation \(\delta\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} [\delta, \ad_X^n] = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \ad_X^k \circ \ad_{\delta(X)} \circ \ad_X^{n-k-1} \text{.} \end{equation*}

The assumption \([\delta(X),X]=0\) implies by the Jacobi identity that the maps \(\ad_{\delta(X)}\) and \(\ad_X\) commute, so the above formula simplifies to

\begin{equation*} [\delta, \ad_X^n] = n \ad_{\delta(X)} \circ \ad_X^n\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(P\) is a polynomial and \(P'\) denotes its derivative polynomial, the above formula implies that

\begin{equation*} [\delta,P(\ad_X)] = \ad_{\delta(X)} \circ P'(\ad_X)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Going to the limit one finds

\begin{equation*} [\delta,e^{-\ad_X}] = \ad_{\delta(-X)} \circ e^{-\ad_X} \end{equation*}

and consequently

\begin{equation*} e^{\ad_X} \circ \delta \circ e^{-\ad_X} = e^{\ad_X} \circ \ad_{\delta(-X)} \circ e^{- \ad_X} + \delta\text{.} \end{equation*}

Recalling that \(\Ad_{\exp(X)} = e^{\ad_X}\text{,}\) see Proposition 1.91 of [22], we conclude using LemmaÂ 4.11 and observing that \(e^{\ad_X}(\delta(X))= \delta(X)\) due to the assumption \([\delta(X),X]=0\text{.}\)
